Multispectral imaging of paintings

You can learn a lot about a painting by simply taking photographs with different types of light.

Visible light sits within the electromagnetic spectrum. This is a contnuous range of wavelengths that ecompass different types of radiation, from very high energy, short wavelength gamma radiation down to low energy, long wavelength microwave and radio waves.
The image below shows where visible light sits on the electromagnetic spectrum; different colours of light have different wavelengths, red being the longest and violet the shortest. (This is why when white light is split up into a rainbow through refraction red is always on the top, and blue always on the bottom).

So how does this help analyse paintings?

This is where multispectral imaging comes in. This invovles taking a variety of pictures of the same object using the different types of frequencies across the electromagnetic spectrum. The wavelengths can be separated by filters that are sensitive to particular wavelengths. Different types of paint and pigments react differently in different types of light, by comparing a set of images taken with light of different energies we can make some interesting assements as to the history and makeup of the painting.

Infra red (830nm)

Infra red has a lower frequency than visible light and it can penetrates deeper into the painting which means that IR images can reveal the under drawing and underlayers of paintings. In this case, it has allowed us to see the detail underneath the cracked as discoloured varnish on the girls dress and hair.

Infra red (1000nm)

Infra red has a lower frequency than visible light and it can penetrates deeper into the painting which means that IR images can reveal the under drawing and underlayers of paintings. 1000 nm (nanometers) is a lower frequency still than 830 nm, which is why there is slightly more detail in this image than the previous one.

Infra red false colour

False colour images are created by splitting a picture taken in visible light into its three different red green blue channels. The red channel is then deleted and replaced by an infra red image. When combined back together this creates an interesting visual image which will highlight details that can only be observed with infra red. The lips and tips of the girls fingers appear orange in this image and pink under UV, this indicates the artist used a pigment called carmine lake.

Find out more about false colour images

Raking light

This is when the painting is illuminated from one side, at an extreme angle to the surface. This reveals the texture of the surface, as raised areas of paint are highglights and create shadow. This image allows you to see the cracking above the girls image an hair, as well as the thick paint layer that was used to create her sleeve. At the top of her shoulder a section of white paint appears to have flaked away, a fact not obvious in any of the other images

UV Flourescence

UV images are paticularly useful for analysing the varnish layers of paintings. On exposure to high energy ultra violet light, some materials will flouresce. Traditional and popular varnishes like mastic and dammar give off a greenish colour. New varnishes will not flouresce as much, and so will appear darker as they prevent. Dyes, especially red ones, flouresce quite strongly in UV light. Can you spot any areas where a red dyes may have been used?

UV Reflectance

UV reflectance is records the radiation that is reflected back from the painting when it is illuminated with an ultraviolet light. New varnish layers will reflect darker, which means touch ups to the painting are much more visible in this light. Can you area of the girls nose that has been restored?

UV False colour

This is similar to Infra red false colour, except instead of replacing the red channel with IR, the blue channel is replaced with UV reflectance.

Transmitted light

This effect is created when the light source is place behind the canvas. This shows where the paint layer is weakest and, in this case, where any significant losses or holes are in the canvas. In this case, there are several areas across the painting which need to be patched up!
